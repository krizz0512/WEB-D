At some point during the afternoon, the sky forgot what color it wanted to be. It shifted between pale blue and dull gray, as if rehearsing for a storm that never arrived. People kept walking faster anyway, carrying umbrellas that stayed closed, driven more by habit than weather. Somewhere between that hesitation and movement, the day quietly passed without making any strong impression.

He liked solving problems that didn’t ask for answers right away. The kind that sat in the back of the mind while he drank tea or stared out of windows. Most people called it overthinking, but to him it felt like tuning an instrument—slow adjustments until clarity appeared. Not everything needed urgency; some things needed space.

The notebook was filled with half-finished ideas, crossed-out sentences, and arrows pointing nowhere. It looked messy, almost careless, but it was honest. Every page captured a moment of curiosity before discipline stepped in. In a strange way, the chaos was proof that something was being built, even if it didn’t yet have a shape.
